If someone had told me a few years ago that I'd be working as a Python backend developer, I probably wouldn't have believed them.

I graduated in Aeronautical Engineering and had no software development experience when I entered the IT industry. When I joined my first company as a Python trainee, everything felt unfamiliar — writing Python code, understanding APIs, working with databases, debugging applications, even reading someone else's code. All of it was completely new to me.

There wasn't a shortcut.

I learned by asking questions, making mistakes, reading documentation, and writing a lot of code. Task by task, project by project, I grew from a trainee into a backend developer. Today, I build backend services for IoT platforms using Python, FastAPI, MongoDB, MQTT, and APIs that power real-world devices. More recently, I've also been working with MCP (Model Context Protocol) servers, building tools that AI applications can use.

Looking back, one thing stands out: every meaningful step in my career started with being willing to learn something I knew nothing about.

Now I feel like I'm standing at another one of those moments.

## The shift I can't ignore

Over the last year, AI has become impossible to ignore.

Every single day, I come across something new. A new LLM is released. Someone shares a new AI tool that promises to improve productivity. Developers are building autonomous agents. Companies are integrating AI into their products. Every week there's another framework, another announcement, another breakthrough.

Whether I'm scrolling through LinkedIn, watching YouTube, exploring GitHub, reading blogs, or simply talking with other developers, the conversation somehow comes back to the same handful of ideas.

Agents.

LLMs.

RAG.

MCP.

It's everywhere.

At first, I wanted to learn AI for a simple reason: I thought it would help my career. As a backend developer, I didn't want to look back a few years from now and realize I had ignored one of the biggest shifts happening in software engineering.

But the more I explored it, the more my reason changed. I stopped seeing AI as just another skill to add to my resume, and started seeing it as a completely different way of building software.

## Why this clicked for me as a backend engineer

What really caught my attention wasn't just the intelligence of the models — it was everything built around them.

A few years ago, building software mostly meant creating APIs, designing databases, handling authentication, writing business logic, and making sure everything scaled reliably. Those things still matter. In fact, they matter even more. But now there's another layer: applications can understand natural language, retrieve information, decide which tools to use, and complete tasks that previously required human interaction.

That shift feels significant.

One of the reasons this became real for me is that I started working with MCP servers. Initially, it was simply another technology I needed to understand for work. But the more I worked with it, the more I realized MCP represents a different way of building applications. Instead of hardcoding every capability into an application, AI models can discover available tools, retrieve the information they need, and decide which actions to perform.

As a backend developer, that immediately clicked with me. Someone still has to build reliable APIs, design secure systems, and think about authentication, databases, observability, monitoring, scalability, and production deployments.

AI doesn't replace backend engineering. It builds on top of it.

That's why I believe backend engineers who understand AI systems will have an important role in the future. Building production-ready AI applications isn't only about calling an LLM API — it's about building reliable systems around those models. That's the direction I want to grow in.

## My commitment for the next six months

So I've made a commitment to myself. For the next six months, I'm going to learn AI engineering the same way I learned backend development.

By building.

Not by collecting certificates. Not by watching endless tutorials. Not by waiting until I feel ready.

I've realized that the fastest way I learn is by creating something, getting stuck, fixing it, and repeating the process. That's exactly how I want to approach AI. I'll be building agent systems, retrieval-based applications, production AI backends, and MCP integrations — and learning how these technologies fit together in real-world software.

Some projects will fail. Some ideas will turn out to be wrong. Some of these blog posts might be about mistakes rather than successes. And I'm completely okay with that.

I'm not trying to become an overnight AI expert. I'm simply a backend developer starting another learning journey — just like I did when I first became a Python trainee.

This blog is part of that commitment. I want to document what I'm learning, what I'm building, what confuses me, what finally makes sense, and the lessons I pick up along the way. Writing helps me organize my thoughts, and sharing the journey publicly gives me a reason to stay consistent.

If you're a backend developer wondering whether you should start learning AI, maybe you'll find something useful here. If you're already experienced in AI, maybe you'll point out things I can improve. Either way, I think learning is better when it's shared.

## Where the journey begins

Over the next few weeks and months, I'll be building AI projects, experimenting with new ideas, exploring agents, MCP, RAG, LLMs, and production AI systems, and writing honestly about everything I learn — the wins, the bugs, the failed experiments, and the lessons that come from all of them.

A few years ago, I had no software development experience and was trying to understand my first Python program. Today, I'm a backend developer taking the first steps into AI engineering. I don't know exactly where this journey will take me — but I've learned that every meaningful chapter in my career started by being willing to learn something new.

This is the beginning of the next one. If you're on a similar journey, I'd love to have you along for the ride.
